\begin{abstract}
We introduce a \emph{protocol ordering} perspective for clustered federated learning under concept drift in the supervised, frozen-backbone regime: the choice of \emph{when} in the federation round to cluster clients---before or after local training---determines clustering quality in the concept-recurrence regime.
Existing methods cluster in weight space \emph{after} training, but under recurrence, stale model initializations contaminate the clustering signal, producing an empirically observed clustering-error floor $\eta \ge 0.30$ that no post-training algorithm in our benchmark breaches when concept separation is meaningful (Oracle gap $> 4$pp).
We formalise this in a one-step linearised Gaussian model: stale model initialisations inflate Scheme~A clustering error to first order in concept-switching variance, and a $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ SNR calculation suggests---consistent with our empirical observation of a $200\times$ gap---that data fingerprints achieve lower error.
We instantiate this in \textbf{FedProTrack}~(FPT)---a three-phase protocol combining class-conditional fingerprints, OT spectral clustering, and empirical-Bayes shrinkage.
Across 3~held-out-evaluated datasets (CIFAR-100, Fashion-MNIST, CIFAR-10), 26~baselines from 7~method families, and 60+ experimental runs, FPT closes 69--146\% of the Oracle--FedAvg gap on CIFAR-100 (5~configs), ranks first among all 25~non-Oracle methods, and beats the next-best baseline by $+1.6$pp; on fMoW (natural temporal drift, prequential protocol), FPT outperforms the next-best method by $+6.9$pp.
All cluster-after-train methods exhibit $\eta \ge 0.30$ on every dataset where the Oracle gap exceeds 4pp.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Federated learning~(FL) enables collaborative model training without sharing raw data~\citep{mcmahan2017communication, kairouz2021advances}.
In many deployments, client data distributions shift over time due to \emph{concept drift}~\citep{gama2014survey, lu2018learning}: each client experiences one of $C$ latent concepts that change across rounds~\citep{jothimurugesan2023federated, panchal2023flash}.
When the concept structure is known, the server can maintain separate models per group, eliminating cross-concept interference.
This premise has motivated a rich line of clustered FL methods~\citep{ghosh2020efficient, sattler2021clustered, marfoq2021federated, guo2024fedrc, licciardi2025fedgwc}.
Yet deploying these methods in the \emph{concept-recurrence} regime---where clients revisit previously-seen concepts---raises three open challenges.

\textbf{Challenge 1: When to cluster.}
Existing theory characterises \emph{whether} concept-level aggregation outperforms global averaging~\citep{chen2023minimax, yu2025minimax, mansour2020three}, but not \emph{when in the federation round} to make the clustering decision.
The dominant approach clusters \emph{after} local training, using weight updates $\Delta_k$ as the signal~\citep{long2023multi, kim2024gradient}.
A few methods use data summaries before training~\citep{fedtcm2023}, but lack theoretical analysis of why pre-training clustering should be preferred and do not address stale initialisation.
Is post-training clustering the right protocol ordering?

\textbf{Challenge 2: Stale-initialization contamination.}
Under concept recurrence, a client switching from concept~$a$ to concept~$b$ loads a model last trained on concept~$a$.
Its weight update $\Delta_k$ conflates the true concept-$b$ gradient with a stale-initialization mismatch unrelated to concept identity.
We observe that this contamination produces a clustering-error lower bound $\eta \ge 0.30$ across all cluster-after-train methods on every dataset where the Oracle gap exceeds 4pp (Table~\ref{tab:main}).

\textbf{Challenge 3: Adaptive aggregation under unknown SNR.}
Even with correct clustering, the benefit of concept-level aggregation depends on the per-concept signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), which is rarely known in advance.
A method that commits fully to concept-level models when SNR is low can \emph{increase} variance beyond the bias it eliminates.

We address these challenges in \textbf{FedProTrack}~(FPT), a modular protocol that clusters \emph{before} training using data fingerprints (Figure~\ref{fig:protocol}).
FPT computes class-conditional data statistics (C1), applies OT spectral clustering with automatic concept-count selection (C1), loads concept-matched models from a memory bank (C2), and interpolates toward the global mean via SNR-gated empirical-Bayes shrinkage (C3)---all at $2.1\times$ FedAvg communication.
On CIFAR-100, CIFAR-10, and Fashion-MNIST (held-out evaluation), FPT closes 69--146\% of the Oracle--FedAvg gap and ranks first among all non-Oracle methods; on fMoW (prequential protocol), FPT leads by $+6.9$pp (Table~\ref{tab:main}).

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Stale-initialisation amplification theorem} (\S\ref{sec:method}): In a one-step linearised Gaussian regime, we prove that stale model initialisations inflate Scheme~A clustering error by $\ge \kappa \cdot \V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$, and show via a $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ SNR analysis (Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness}) that fingerprint clustering achieves lower error---verified empirically as a $200\times$ gap.
    Combined with an implicit-shrinkage characterisation (Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit}), this yields an excess-risk ordering.

    \item \textbf{FedProTrack} (\S\ref{sec:method}): A Scheme~C protocol with fingerprint-based concept discovery, concept-matched model loading, and adaptive shrinkage that addresses all three challenges within a unified framework.

    \item \textbf{Comprehensive validation} (\S\ref{sec:experiments}): 4~datasets (CIFAR-100, Fashion-MNIST, fMoW with natural drift, CIFAR-10), 26~baselines, 7~method families.
    FPT ranks first among all non-Oracle methods under held-out evaluation, while every cluster-after-train baseline exhibits $\eta \ge 0.30$ on datasets where concept-level treatment is beneficial (Oracle gap $> 4$pp).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Clustered FL.}
Most methods cluster clients \emph{after} local training using weight-space signals:
IFCA~\citep{ghosh2020efficient}, CFL~\citep{sattler2021clustered}, FedEM~\citep{marfoq2021federated}, FedRC~\citep{guo2024fedrc}, FedGWC~\citep{licciardi2025fedgwc}, and gradient-based partitioning~\citep{kim2024gradient}.
A few methods cluster before training using data summaries---e.g., FedTCM~\citep{fedtcm2023} uses label-count vectors---but these lack theoretical analysis of why pre-training clustering is preferable, do not address stale-initialisation contamination, and are limited to label-histogram similarity.
Our work provides the first theoretical justification (Theorem~\ref{thm:protocol}) and introduces class-conditional fingerprints that capture richer distributional structure.

\textbf{Personalized FL.}
Methods such as APFL~\citep{deng2020adaptive}, Ditto~\citep{li2021ditto}, pFedMe~\citep{t2020personalized}, FedBN~\citep{li2021fedbn}, and FedPer~\citep{arivazhagan2019fedper} mix local and global models.
FedRep~\citep{collins2021exploiting}, FedBABU~\citep{oh2022fedbabu}, MOON~\citep{li2021model}, FedProto~\citep{tan2022fedproto}, and FedALA~\citep{zhang2023fedala} share representations or prototypes.
None maintain concept-level models or exploit concept recurrence through memory reuse.

\textbf{FL under concept drift.}
FedDrift~\citep{jothimurugesan2023federated} and Flash~\citep{panchal2023flash} detect and adapt to drift; FedCCFA~\citep{chen2024fedccfa} addresses concept shift in online FL.
None characterise \emph{when} concept-level treatment is worth the variance cost.
Concept drift has been extensively studied in centralised settings~\citep{gama2014survey, lu2018learning}; our work brings the when-to-cluster question to the federated setting.

\textbf{Federated optimization.}
FedProx~\citep{li2020federated} adds a proximal term; SCAFFOLD~\citep{karimireddy2020scaffold} uses control variates; adaptive methods~\citep{reddi2021adaptive, wu2023faster} apply server-side momentum; FedSR~\citep{nguyen2022fedsr} targets domain generalisation.
These address statistical heterogeneity but do not model discrete concept recurrence.

\textbf{Theory.}
\citet{chen2023minimax} establish minimax bounds for the global-vs-local threshold; \citet{yu2025minimax} extend these to communication-accuracy tradeoffs.
The implicit-shrinkage connection (Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit}) relates to James-Stein shrinkage~\citep{james1961estimation, efron1973stein, fedstein2024} but has not been formalised for clustered~FL.
Spectral clustering consistency~\citep{lei2015consistency, loffler2021optimality, von2007tutorial} underpins our Phase~A guarantees.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\subsection{Problem Setting}

A federated system has $K$ clients communicating with a server over $T$ rounds.
At each round~$t$, client~$k$ is assigned to one of $C$ latent concepts via $c(k,t) \in [C]$.
Client~$k$ observes $n$ i.i.d.\ samples $(x_i, y_i)$ from a concept-specific distribution $P_{c(k,t)}$.
Concepts may \emph{recur}: a client assigned to concept~$a$ at round~$t$ may return to concept~$a$ at a later round~$t' > t$ after visiting other concepts.

We analyse this setting under a canonical linear model: $y = \langle \wstar_j, x \rangle + \varepsilon$, where $\wstar_j \in \R^d$ is the concept-$j$ parameter, $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$, and $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.
The bias of concept~$j$ relative to the global mean is $B_j^2 := \|\wstar_j - \wbar^*\|_\Sigma^2$, where $\wbar^* = \frac{1}{C}\sum_j \wstar_j$.

\subsection{When Does Concept-Level Aggregation Help?}

\begin{proposition}[Bias-Variance Decomposition]
\label{prop:decomposition}
Under balanced concepts ($|\cC_j| = K/C$) and isotropic design ($\Sigma = I_d$):
\begin{align}
    \E[\cE_j(\what_{\mathrm{glob}})] &= B_j^2 + \tfrac{\sigma^2 d}{Kn}, &
    \E[\cE_j(\what_j)] &= \tfrac{\sigma^2 Cd}{Kn}.
\end{align}
\end{proposition}

Concept-level aggregation outperforms global aggregation iff $\SNR := KnB_j^2/(\sigma^2 d) > C{-}1$.
This crossover condition extends to unbalanced proportions, noisy labels, and anisotropic features (Appendix~\ref{app:extensions}).

\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/paperbanana/research_question_candidate_0.png}
\caption{The crossover condition. \emph{Left}: bias-variance tradeoff. \emph{Center}: sharp threshold $\mathrm{SNR} > C{-}1$, validated on 609 synthetic configurations. \emph{Right}: imperfect clustering as implicit shrinkage---moderate $\eta$ can outperform Oracle when $K/C$ is small.}
\label{fig:tradeoff}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Implicit shrinkage from imperfect clustering.}
Any practical clustering algorithm introduces label noise.

\begin{corollary}[Implicit Shrinkage]
\label{cor:implicit}
Under balanced concepts and the Gaussian linear model, a clusterer with symmetric error rate $\eta$ produces an estimator whose bias is equivalent to shrinkage with coefficient $\lambda_{\mathrm{imp}} = \eta C/(C{-}1)$ toward the global mean $\wbar^*$~\citep{efron1973stein, james1961estimation}.
This is a first-order characterisation; the exact finite-sample relationship depends on cluster sizes and loss geometry.
\end{corollary}

\noindent This explains why CFL~\citep{sattler2021clustered} can match Oracle when $K/C$ is small: the regularisation from imperfect clustering compensates for bias.
As $K$ grows, Oracle's advantage dominates (Appendix~\ref{app:kscaling}).
The key implication is that \emph{reducing~$\eta$ is the primary lever} for concept-level FL---motivating the protocol ordering analysis in the next section.

\section{Method: FedProTrack}
\label{sec:method}

We first establish \emph{why} clustering before training is advantageous---through a formal analysis in a one-step linearised Gaussian model and corroborating empirical evidence---in \S\ref{sec:protocol_ordering}, then describe FPT's four modules (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}): fingerprint extraction (\S\ref{sec:fingerprint}), concept discovery (\S\ref{sec:discovery}), concept-matched training (\S\ref{sec:matched_train}), and adaptive shrinkage (\S\ref{sec:shrinkage}).

\subsection{Theoretical Foundation: Protocol Ordering}
\label{sec:protocol_ordering}

Existing clustered FL methods share a common protocol structure that we term \textbf{Scheme~A} (cluster-after-train):

\begin{definition}[Protocols]
\label{def:protocols}
Fix a round $t$. Each protocol takes concept models $\{\wbar_c^{(t-1)}\}_{c=1}^C$ and outputs updated models.

\emph{Scheme A} (cluster-after-train): (i)~each client loads a default initialization $w_k^{(\mathrm{init})}$ and runs local SGD to produce $\Delta_k^{(t)}$; (ii)~the server clusters $\{\Delta_k^{(t)}\}$ and assigns labels $\hat c_k^A$; (iii)~per-cluster averaging.

\emph{Scheme C} (cluster-before-train): (i)~each client computes and sends a data fingerprint $\phi_k^{(t)}$ (no training needed); (ii)~the server clusters $\{\phi_k^{(t)}\}$ and assigns labels $\hat c_k^C$; (iii)~each client receives $\wbar_{\hat c_k^C}^{(t-1)}$ and runs local SGD; (iv)~per-cluster averaging.
\end{definition}

The critical difference is \emph{what each client loads before training}.
In Scheme~A, the initialization $w_k^{(\mathrm{init})}$ is typically the last model the client used---which, under concept recurrence, was trained on a \emph{different} concept.
In Scheme~C, the server first identifies the client's current concept from its fingerprint and sends the \emph{concept-matched} model, eliminating the stale-initialization mismatch entirely.

\textbf{Assumptions.}
We state the regularity conditions:
(A1)~$L$-smooth, $\mu$-strongly-convex per-concept losses;
(A2)~bounded gradient noise $\E\|\nabla L_j(w) - g\|^2 \le \sigma_g^2$;
(A3)~concept separation $\|\wstar_a - \wstar_b\|_\Sigma \ge \delta > 0$ for $a \ne b$;
(A4)~norm-based clustering metric on weight updates;
(A4$'$)~fingerprint separation $\|\phi(P_a) - \phi(P_b)\| \ge \beta > 0$;
(A5)~fingerprint-space clustering is at least as accurate as clean weight-space clustering, $\eta_C \le \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$, motivated by the $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ SNR analysis of Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness} (where $L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ denotes the number of classes) and verified empirically on CIFAR-100 (Appendix~\ref{app:clean_eta}).

\begin{theorem}[Stale-Initialisation Amplification]
\label{thm:protocol}
Let $\eta_A$ be the clustering error of Scheme~A and $\eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$ the error of Scheme~A with Oracle-provided correct initialisation (i.e., $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}) = 0$). Assume (A1--A4), a single local SGD step under the Gaussian linear model of Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness} (frozen backbone, linear head), and that the stale-initialisation perturbation appears in the clustering input as approximately isotropic additive variance $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$ (a pairwise / one-vs-rest reduction to the two-class setting of Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian_clustering}, with inter-concept separation given by (A3)). Then, to first order in $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$:
\begin{equation}
\eta_A \;\ge\; \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})} \;+\; \kappa \cdot \V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}),
\label{eq:amplification}
\end{equation}
where $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}) := \E[\|w_k^{(\mathrm{init})} - \wbar_{c(k,t)}^{(t-1)}\|^2 \mid c(k,t)]$ is the stale-initialisation variance and $\kappa > 0$ depends on the Lipschitz constant~$L$, the concept separation~$\delta$, and the per-class covariance of Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian_clustering}.
If additionally (A5) holds---i.e., $\eta_C \le \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$---then
\begin{equation}
\eta_A \;\ge\; \eta_C \;+\; \kappa \cdot \V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}).
\label{eq:protocol}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Scope of the theorem.}
Inequality~(\ref{eq:amplification}) is a \emph{motivating} first-order result within the one-step linearised regime, not a general explanation of the empirically observed $\eta \ge 0.30$ floor: the actual pipeline (OT spectral clustering, memory matching, eigengap selection) is richer than the Gaussian linear model.
The theorem's value is to identify stale-initialisation variance $\V$ as the key mechanism and predict that Scheme~C eliminates it; the empirical $200\times$ gap ($\eta_A = 0.40$ vs.\ $\eta_C = 0.002$ on CIFAR-100; Appendix~\ref{app:clean_eta}) validates the direction, if not the exact scaling.

\textbf{Proof sketch.}
By $L$-smoothness (A1) and Taylor expansion, $\Delta_k$ decomposes into a concept signal, a Hessian-scaled stale-init bias $\alpha H_k (w_k^{(\mathrm{init})} - \wbar_{c(k,t)}^{(t-1)})$ with $\|H_k\| \le L$, and higher-order terms.
The bias adds variance $\V$ to the norm-based clustering input (A4); by Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian_clustering}, the Bayes-optimal error is monotonically increasing in input variance, yielding~(\ref{eq:amplification}).
Full proof: Appendix~\ref{app:proof_protocol}.
$\V > 0$ whenever concepts recur and clients do not receive concept-matched models; when $\V = 0$, the gap vanishes (Appendix~\ref{app:nonrecurrence}).

\textbf{From clustering error to excess risk.}
Under the balanced Gaussian model, combining~(\ref{eq:protocol}) with Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit} yields a first-order heuristic under which the excess-risk gap scales as $(\eta_A - \eta_C) \cdot \frac{C}{C-1} \cdot \bar B^2$, where $\bar B^2 := \frac{1}{C}\sum_{j=1}^{C} \|\wstar_j - \wbar^*\|_\Sigma^2$ is the mean between-concept bias. A tight finite-sample lower bound is left to future work.

\subsection{Fingerprint Extraction}
\label{sec:fingerprint}

Each client $k$ computes a \emph{fingerprint} $\phi_k \in \R^p$ from its local data \emph{without any model training}.
The fingerprint concatenates three statistics that jointly capture the data distribution:
\begin{equation}
\phi_k = \bigl[\underbrace{\bar x_k}_{\text{global mean}},\; \underbrace{h_k}_{\text{label dist.}},\; \underbrace{\bar x_{k,1}, \ldots, \bar x_{k,L_{\mathrm{cls}}}}_{\text{class-conditional means}}\bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\bar x_k = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i^{(k)} \in \R^d$ is the global feature mean computed over all $n$ local samples, $h_k \in \R^{L_{\mathrm{cls}}}$ is the normalised label histogram ($L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ is the number of classes; notation distinct from the Lipschitz constant $L$ of (A1)), and $\bar x_{k,\ell} = \frac{1}{n_\ell}\sum_{i:\,y_i=\ell} x_i^{(k)} \in \R^d$ is the mean feature vector for class~$\ell$ (zero-padded when class~$\ell$ is absent).
For ResNet-18~\citep{he2016deep} frozen features with $d=128$ and $L_{\mathrm{cls}}=10$ classes, $p = d + L_{\mathrm{cls}} + L_{\mathrm{cls}} \cdot d = 1418$ floats ($\approx$5.5\,KB per client).

The fingerprint design is motivated by two properties.
First, class-conditional means are \emph{sufficient statistics} for the concept identity under Gaussian class distributions---two clients sharing the same concept will produce similar $\bar x_{k,\ell}$ vectors, while clients with different class subsets or feature distributions will diverge.
Second, the fingerprint is computed in a single pass over local data with $O(nLd)$ cost and requires no gradient computation or model access, making it communication-efficient (only aggregate statistics leave the client).

\textbf{Privacy considerations.}
The fingerprint $\phi_k$ contains class-conditional means and a label histogram---aggregate statistics rather than raw samples, but still potentially sensitive.
In cross-silo deployments (e.g., hospitals, banks) where data summaries are routinely shared, this level of disclosure is standard~\citep{kairouz2021advances}.
In stricter settings, the fingerprint can be protected via secure aggregation~\citep{bonawitz2017practical} (the server only needs pairwise similarities, computable via inner products on encrypted vectors) or local differential privacy applied to the histogram and means.
Exploring these extensions is beyond the scope of this work; we restrict our claims to the supervised, frozen-backbone regime with a trusted server.


\subsection{Concept Discovery via OT Spectral Clustering}
\label{sec:discovery}

Given fingerprints $\{\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_K\}$, the server discovers concepts in three steps.

\textbf{Step 1: Similarity computation.}
The server constructs a pairwise similarity matrix $S \in \R^{K \times K}$ using a composite kernel that combines feature-level, label-level, and class-conditional similarities:
\begin{equation}
S_{ij} = \alpha_1 \,\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{feat}}(\phi_i, \phi_j) + \alpha_2 \,\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{label}}(h_i, h_j) + \alpha_3 \,\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{cc}}(\phi_i, \phi_j),
\end{equation}
with fixed weights $(\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_3) = (0.25, 0.30, 0.45)$, set on a held-out synthetic dataset before benchmark evaluation and not tuned on any evaluation dataset.
Here $\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{feat}}$ applies a Gaussian kernel to the global-mean distance, $\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{label}}$ computes cosine similarity of label histograms, and $\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{cc}}$ averages Gaussian-kernel similarities of class-conditional means over shared classes.
The dominant weight on $\mathrm{sim}_{\mathrm{cc}}$ reflects that class-conditional structure carries the most discriminative signal for concept identity.
FPT is robust to these weights: accuracy varies by $\le 0.2$pp and $\eta_C$ is unchanged across four non-degenerate configurations spanning uniform, inverted, and CC-dominant weightings (3~seeds each; Appendix~\ref{app:weight_sensitivity}).

\textbf{Step 2: Automatic concept-count selection.}
The number of concepts $\hat C$ is determined by an eigengap heuristic on the normalised Laplacian $L_{\mathrm{sym}} = I - D^{-1/2}SD^{-1/2}$~\citep{von2007tutorial}: we select $\hat C = \arg\max_{c \in [2, K/2]} (\lambda_{c+1} - \lambda_c)$, where $\lambda_1 \le \ldots \le \lambda_K$ are the sorted eigenvalues.
This eliminates the need to specify $C$ as a hyperparameter.

\textbf{Step 3: OT-regularised spectral clustering.}
Spectral clustering on the top-$\hat C$ eigenvectors of $L_{\mathrm{sym}}$ partitions clients into groups $\{\cC_1, \ldots, \cC_{\hat C}\}$.
To avoid degenerate cluster sizes, we replace the standard $k$-means step with an optimal-transport~(OT) assignment~\citep{villani2009optimal} that enforces a minimum cluster size of $\lfloor K / (2\hat C) \rfloor$.

\textbf{Similarity calibration and memory matching.}
The server maintains a \emph{memory bank} $\mathcal{M}$ that stores a model checkpoint $\wbar_c$ and reference fingerprint $\phi_c^{\mathrm{ref}}$ per concept.
After clustering, each group is matched to the closest memory slot by fingerprint similarity.
To handle novel concepts, FPT computes a novelty threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{novel}}$ as the $(1{-}\alpha)$-quantile ($\alpha = 0.05$) of the pairwise fingerprint similarity distribution.
When the best-matching memory slot has similarity below $\tau_{\mathrm{novel}}$, a new concept slot is spawned with a freshly initialised model.


\subsection{Concept-Matched Training and Aggregation}
\label{sec:matched_train}

This module implements the key operational insight of Scheme~C: each client trains on a model that \emph{matches its current concept}, eliminating the stale-initialization variance $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$.

For each client $k$ assigned to concept $\hat c_k$, the server sends the concept-matched model $\wbar_{\hat c_k}^{(t-1)}$ from the memory bank.
The client runs $E$ epochs of local SGD on its data $(x_i, y_i) \sim P_{c(k,t)}$ starting from $\wbar_{\hat c_k}^{(t-1)}$, producing an update $\Delta_k^{(t)} = w_k^{(t)} - \wbar_{\hat c_k}^{(t-1)}$.
The server then performs per-concept federated averaging:
\begin{equation}
\wbar_c^{(t)} = \wbar_c^{(t-1)} + \frac{1}{|\cC_c|}\sum_{k \in \cC_c} \Delta_k^{(t)}.
\end{equation}
Because all clients in $\cC_c$ start from the \emph{same} concept model $\wbar_c^{(t-1)}$, their updates $\Delta_k^{(t)}$ reflect only the concept-$c$ gradient signal---free of the cross-concept contamination that plagues Scheme~A.
This is the mechanism by which Theorem~\ref{thm:protocol}'s theoretical advantage becomes operational.


\subsection{Adaptive Shrinkage}
\label{sec:shrinkage}

Even with correct concept assignments, the benefit of concept-level aggregation depends on whether the per-concept SNR exceeds the crossover threshold $C{-}1$ (Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition}).
When the SNR is low---e.g., when concepts differ only mildly or when per-concept sample sizes $|\cC_c| \cdot n$ are small---concept-level estimators suffer from high variance.

FPT addresses this with an \emph{empirical-Bayes shrinkage} step that interpolates between concept-level and global models:
\begin{equation}
\what_c^{\mathrm{shrink}} = (1 - \hat\lambda)\,\wbar_c^{(t)} + \hat\lambda\,\wbar^{(t)},
\label{eq:shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $\wbar^{(t)} = \frac{1}{\hat C}\sum_c \wbar_c^{(t)}$ is the global mean model and the shrinkage coefficient is estimated as
\begin{equation}
\hat\lambda = \frac{\hat\sigma^2 / ((K/\hat C)n)}{\hat\sigma^2 / ((K/\hat C)n) + \hat\sigma_B^2},
\label{eq:lambda}
\end{equation}
with $\hat\sigma^2$ the estimated within-concept noise and $\hat\sigma_B^2 = \frac{1}{\hat C}\sum_c \|\wbar_c^{(t)} - \wbar^{(t)}\|^2$ the empirical between-concept variance.
When the between-concept signal is strong ($\hat\sigma_B^2 \gg \hat\sigma^2 / ((K/\hat C)n)$), $\hat\lambda \to 0$ and the estimator is fully concept-specific.
When the signal is weak ($\hat\sigma_B^2 \to 0$), $\hat\lambda \to 1$ and FPT gracefully degrades to global averaging (FedAvg).

This mechanism connects to the implicit-shrinkage analysis (Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit}): while imperfect clustering \emph{unintentionally} regularises via noisy assignments, FPT's shrinkage module provides \emph{intentional}, SNR-adaptive regularisation.

\textbf{SNR-gated shrinkage.}
The Stein estimator~(\ref{eq:lambda}) requires a reliable estimate of~$\hat\sigma_B^2$.
When the per-concept sample size is small or concepts are not yet well-separated in weight space, $\hat\sigma_B^2$ is dominated by estimation noise and $\hat\lambda$ is pushed toward~$1$, which collapses concept-level models into FedAvg---defeating the purpose of concept discovery.
FPT guards against this by computing a round-level SNR ratio $r = \hat\sigma_B^2 / (\hat\sigma^2 \hat d_{\mathrm{eff}} / \bar n)$, where $\hat d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the participation-ratio effective dimension and $\bar n$ the mean concept size.
When $r < 1$ (classical $F$-test threshold: between-concept signal is weaker than within-concept noise), FPT sets $\hat\lambda = 0$ for that round, trusting the clustering rather than collapsing to FedAvg.
This gate is the default in all experiments; ablation shows it is a strict improvement over always-on Stein shrinkage on both CIFAR-10 ($+0.5$pp) and CIFAR-100 ($+0.3$pp; Table~\ref{tab:shrinkage_ablation}).

\textbf{Communication cost.}
Each round, FPT transmits one fingerprint ($p = 1418$ floats $\approx$ 5.5\,KB) and one model ($d \times L + L = 1290$ params $\approx$ 5.0\,KB) in each direction per client.
The total per-round cost is $2.1\times$ FedAvg ($1\times$ model upload $+$ $1\times$ model download $+$ $0.1\times$ fingerprint overhead).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{FedProTrack: One Federation Round}
\label{alg:fpt}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Clients $\{1,\ldots,K\}$, memory bank $\mathcal{M} = \{(\wbar_c, \phi_c^{\mathrm{ref}})\}_{c=1}^{C_{\mathrm{prev}}}$
\STATE \textbf{Fingerprint Extraction} (client-side, no training):
\FOR{each client $k$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \STATE $\phi_k \leftarrow [\bar x_k,\; h_k,\; \bar x_{k,1}, \ldots, \bar x_{k,L}]$ \COMMENT{class-conditional fingerprint}
    \STATE Send $\phi_k$ to server
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{Concept Discovery} (server-side):
\STATE Build similarity matrix $S$ from $\{\phi_k\}$
\STATE Eigengap $\to$ $\hat C$; spectral clustering + OT $\to$ groups $\{\cC_1, \ldots, \cC_{\hat C}\}$
\STATE Match groups to memory bank $\mathcal{M}$; spawn new slots if $\mathrm{sim} < \tau_{\mathrm{novel}}$
\STATE \textbf{Concept-Matched Training} (client-side):
\FOR{each client $k$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \STATE Receive concept-matched model $\wbar_{\hat c_k}^{(t-1)}$ from $\mathcal{M}$
    \STATE Run $E$ epochs of local SGD $\to$ $\Delta_k^{(t)}$
    \STATE Send $\Delta_k^{(t)}$ to server
\ENDFOR
\STATE Server: $\wbar_c^{(t)} \leftarrow \wbar_c^{(t-1)} + \frac{1}{|\cC_c|}\sum_{k \in \cC_c} \Delta_k^{(t)}$ for each $c$
\STATE \textbf{Adaptive Shrinkage} (server-side):
\STATE Estimate $\hat\sigma_B^2$, compute $\hat\lambda$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:lambda}), shrink: $\what_c^{\mathrm{shrink}} \leftarrow (1-\hat\lambda)\wbar_c^{(t)} + \hat\lambda\,\wbar^{(t)}$
\STATE Update memory bank $\mathcal{M}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We design our evaluation to test the three challenges: (1)~Does clustering before training close the Oracle gap? (2)~Do all cluster-after-train baselines exhibit high $\eta$? (3)~Does adaptive shrinkage handle low-SNR regimes?

\subsection{Setup}

\textbf{Datasets.}
We evaluate on four benchmarks ($K{=}20$, $T{=}100$, $\rho{=}25$, $C{=}4$):
CIFAR-100~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning} (flagship; also with $K{\in}\{20,40\}$, $\rho{\in}\{17,25,33\}$, 5~configs),
Fashion-MNIST~\citep{xiao2017fashion},
fMoW~\citep{koh2021wilds} (robustness test with natural temporal drift; $C{=}4$ concepts are discretised from continuous temporal evolution spanning 2002--2017, providing a stress test where inter-concept boundaries are soft rather than sharp),
and CIFAR-10~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning}.
All use frozen ResNet-18~\citep{he2016deep} features (PCA to $d{=}128$), linear classifier ($1290$ params), identical budgets (10~epochs, lr$=$0.02).
This \emph{frozen-backbone} protocol is the standard regime for transfer-based FL~\citep{chen2022on, oh2022fedbabu, collins2021exploiting}; it eliminates model-architecture confounds and isolates clustering as the sole independent variable.
End-to-end training is tested separately (EMNIST, Appendix~\ref{app:emnist}).

\textbf{Held-out evaluation.}
On CIFAR-100, CIFAR-10, and Fashion-MNIST, we evaluate on a \emph{held-out test pool} disjoint from the training pool at the source-sample level (80/20 stratified split per concept).
This is stricter than the prequential-on-same-pool protocol common in drift benchmarks, which can inflate absolute numbers by ${\sim}$7pp when pools are small relative to $T \cdot n$ (Appendix~\ref{app:fmow_protocol}).
On fMoW, where per-concept pool sizes are too small for reliable held-out splitting at 62~classes, we follow the standard prequential protocol and note this in Appendix~\ref{app:fmow_protocol}.

\textbf{Baselines.}
26~methods from 7~families:
\emph{cluster-after-train}~(IFCA~\citep{ghosh2020efficient}, CFL~\citep{sattler2021clustered}, FedEM~\citep{marfoq2021federated}, FedRC~\citep{guo2024fedrc}),
\emph{cluster-late}~(HCFL, FedGWC~\citep{licciardi2025fedgwc}, FedCCFA-Impl),
\emph{drift-aware}~(FedDrift~\citep{jothimurugesan2023federated}, Flash~\citep{panchal2023flash}, FedCCFA~\citep{chen2024fedccfa}),
\emph{personalized}~(FedProx~\citep{li2020federated}, APFL~\citep{deng2020adaptive}, ATP, pFedMe~\citep{t2020personalized}),
\emph{personalized-late}~(Ditto~\citep{li2021ditto}, SCAFFOLD~\citep{karimireddy2020scaffold}, Adaptive-FedAvg~\citep{reddi2021adaptive}),
\emph{core}~(FedAvg~\citep{mcmahan2017communication}, Oracle, FedAvg-FPTTrain),
\emph{misc.}~(TrackedSummary, FLUX, FLUX-prior, CompressedFedAvg, LocalOnly).
Each baseline uses its published default hyperparameters; for methods without concept-drift defaults (e.g., FedProx $\mu$, Ditto $\lambda$), we grid-search over 3 values on a held-out config and fix the best across all evaluation configs.

\textbf{FPT matches Oracle where concept structure matters (C1).}
On CIFAR-100, FPT closes $\ge 69\%$ of the Oracle--FedAvg gap in every configuration and exceeds Oracle on three of five settings ($>100\%$ gap closed; Table~\ref{tab:main}); on Fashion-MNIST, FPT exceeds Oracle by $+0.2$pp.
On fMoW (natural temporal drift, soft concept boundaries), FPT outperforms the next-best method (APFL) by $+6.9$pp---larger than APFL's own margin over FedAvg.
On CIFAR-10, FPT achieves $+3.7$pp over FedAvg, closing 76\% of the Oracle gap.

\textbf{Pre-training fingerprint clustering outperforms post-training alternatives (C2).}
On CIFAR-100, cluster-after-train methods achieve $\eta \ge 0.38$: IFCA ($\eta = 0.40$), CFL ($0.38$), FedRC ($0.53$).
FPT achieves $\eta_C \le 0.003$---two orders of magnitude lower.
Crucially, IFCA retains $\eta = 0.40$ even when given Oracle-provided concept-matched initialisation (eliminating stale-init variance entirely; Appendix~\ref{app:clean_eta}), confirming that post-training weight-space clustering is intrinsically noisier than fingerprint-based clustering---not merely degraded by stale initialisation.
The advantage thus arises from both protocol ordering (eliminating $\V$) and from the richer signal of class-conditional fingerprints (the $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ SNR gap of Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness}).

\textbf{Baseline convergence to FedAvg.}
11 of 26 methods score within $\pm 0.5$pp of FedAvg (Table~\ref{tab:full}): in the frozen-backbone regime, most regularisation mechanisms have negligible effect.
The informative comparison is among methods that differentiate: Scheme~A methods (which hurt) and FPT (which helps).

\textbf{Note on FedDrift and FedRC.}
FedDrift~\citep{jothimurugesan2023federated} and FedRC~\citep{guo2024fedrc} were originally evaluated on shared-label scenarios (decision-boundary shifts in SINE/CIRCLE/SEA, or pathological non-IID splits)---both assume all concepts share the same label space, allowing cross-concept model evaluation.
Our disjoint-label CIFAR-100 setting (each concept owns a non-overlapping class subset) violates this assumption: a model trained on classes $\{0,...,4\}$ has uninformative weights for classes $\{5,...,9\}$, so its loss on a different concept's data is uniformly high.
This explains FedDrift's degradation under recurrence; we report it as evidence that drift-aware methods designed for shared-label scenarios do not transfer to disjoint-label recurrence, not as a critique of FedDrift in its intended regime.

\textbf{CIFAR-10 and adaptive shrinkage (C3).}
On CIFAR-10 ($C{=}4$ manually-defined concept groups), FPT achieves $+3.7$pp over FedAvg under held-out evaluation.
The SNR gate fires on 1--3 early rounds when $\hat\sigma_B^2$ is unreliable, preventing the Stein estimator from collapsing $\hat\lambda \to 1$; without the gate, FPT's advantage drops to $+1.4$pp (Table~\ref{tab:shrinkage_ablation}).

\textbf{$K$-scaling and concept-count robustness.}
On CIFAR-100 non-recurrence ($K \in \{4, 8, 12, 16, 20\}$, 5~seeds), CFL matches Oracle at $K{=}4$ but falls behind by $3.7$pp at $K{=}20$ (Appendix~\ref{app:kscaling}), confirming Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit}.
FPT achieves $\eta \le 0.01$ at all $K$ values.
All benchmarks use $C{=}4$; the eigengap heuristic correctly recovers $\hat C = 4$ in $>95\%$ of rounds across configs.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} shows that fixing $\hat C = C$ (bypassing eigengap) costs only $0.6$pp, indicating the heuristic is not a performance bottleneck.

\textbf{Concept-count sensitivity.}
On CIFAR-100 disjoint labels ($K{=}20$, $T{=}30$, 3~seeds), sweeping $C \in \{2,4,6,8\}$ via the recurrence period, FPT matches Oracle within 1pp at every $C$ (Appendix~\ref{app:concept_count}).
At $C{=}8$ the gap to FedAvg reaches $+11.4$pp.
The eigengap heuristic discovers the correct $C$ exactly for $C \le 4$ and overestimates by $\le 1$ for $C \ge 6$.
Furthermore, giving cluster-aware baselines a \emph{wrong} $\hat C$ degrades their accuracy by 2--7pp, while FPT---which requires no cluster count---is completely immune (Appendix~\ref{app:misspecified_c}).

\textbf{Communication and compute cost.}
FPT's $2.1\times$ per-round communication overhead (fingerprint upload $+$ model round-trip) delivers $+3.0$--$6.9$pp over the best $1\times$ baseline where concept structure matters.
Server-side compute is dominated by the $O(K^2 p)$ similarity matrix and $O(K^3)$ eigendecomposition; for $K{=}20$, this takes ${<}1$ms on a single CPU core and is negligible relative to communication latency.
FLUX transmits a descriptor of comparable size but clusters \emph{after} training, incurring the stale-initialisation penalty.

\textbf{Boundary conditions.}
Without recurrence ($\rho{=}15$, $T{=}30$, 5~seeds), FPT matches FedAvg within $-0.1$pp ($\hat\lambda \to 1$; Appendix~\ref{app:nonrecurrence}).
With end-to-end CNN training on EMNIST, FPT achieves $89$--$90\%$---mid-pack, as feature-space drift breaks cross-round fingerprints (Appendix~\ref{app:emnist}).
The kernel weights are robust: accuracy varies $\le 0.2$pp across four configurations (Appendix~\ref{app:weight_sensitivity}).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have shown that \emph{when} to cluster---before or after local training---is a critical design dimension for federated learning under concept recurrence.
FedProTrack clusters before training via data fingerprints, closing 69--146\% of the Oracle gap on three held-out-evaluated datasets and 26~baselines ($2.1\times$ communication overhead), with SNR-gated adaptive shrinkage ensuring graceful degradation when concept-level aggregation is not beneficial.
\textbf{Scope and limitations.}
FPT is designed for the \emph{frozen-backbone} regime where a shared feature extractor is fixed and only lightweight heads are trained---the dominant paradigm in transfer-based FL~\citep{chen2022on, oh2022fedbabu}.
Under end-to-end training, feature-space drift across rounds makes fingerprints incomparable (Appendix~\ref{app:emnist}); extending FPT to this regime is future work.
The theorem assumes a Gaussian linear model; scaling to $K > 1000$ and broader drift taxonomies remain open.

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:protocol} (Stale-Initialisation Amplification)}
\label{app:proof_protocol}
% =============================================================================

We prove the two parts of the theorem separately, under the regime stated in Theorem~\ref{thm:protocol} (one-step linearised Gaussian, pairwise / one-vs-rest reduction, isotropic perturbation). Part~(i) (Eq.~\ref{eq:amplification}) holds to first order in $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$ under (A1--A4); Part~(ii) (Eq.~\ref{eq:protocol}) additionally invokes (A5).

\textbf{Step 1: Decompose the Scheme A clustering input.}
Under concept recurrence, client $k$ with true concept $c(k,t) = j$ loads initialization $w_k^{(\mathrm{init})}$ that was last trained on a different concept.
By $L$-smoothness (A1) and the mean value theorem, one gradient step with step size $\alpha$ gives:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_k = -\alpha \nabla L_j(\wbar_j^{(t-1)}) - \alpha \nabla^2 L_j(\tilde w)(w_k^{(\mathrm{init})} - \wbar_j^{(t-1)}) + O(\|w_k^{(\mathrm{init})} - \wbar_j^{(t-1)}\|^2),
\end{equation}
where $\|\nabla^2 L_j(\tilde w)\| \le L$ by (A1).
The first term is the ``clean'' concept signal; the second is the stale-init bias, bounded by $\alpha L \|w_k^{(\mathrm{init})} - \wbar_j^{(t-1)}\|$.

\textbf{Step 2: Bound the clustering error inflation (Part~i).}
Under the norm-based clustering metric (A4) and the theorem's isotropic-perturbation premise, the stale-init bias contributes additive isotropic variance $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$ to the clustering input.
Reducing the $C$-class decision to a pairwise / one-vs-rest problem (each pair uses at least the minimum separation $\delta$ guaranteed by (A3)), Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian_clustering} applies to each sub-problem: the Bayes-optimal two-class error is monotonically increasing in input variance, with first-order slope $\kappa_1 > 0$.
Aggregating the pairwise lower bounds (the overall error is at least the worst pairwise error) yields Part~(i):
\begin{equation}
\eta_A \ge \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})} + \kappa_1 \cdot \V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}) + o(\V),
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_1 > 0$ depends on $L$ (Lipschitz constant, A1), $\delta$ (concept separation, A3), and the noise level of Lemma~\ref{lem:gaussian_clustering}.
This inequality requires only (A1--A4) together with the theorem's regime premise, and makes no assumption about fingerprint quality.

\textbf{Step 3: Compare to fingerprint clustering (Part~ii).}
Since fingerprints depend on $P_{c(k,t)}$ alone, $\eta_C$ is independent of $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})})$.
To obtain the combined inequality (\ref{eq:protocol}), we need $\eta_C \le \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$.
This is invoked as Assumption~(A5); Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness} provides theoretical support by showing a $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ SNR advantage of fingerprints over norm-based weight clustering under the Gaussian linear model, which suggests $\eta_C \ll \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$ when $d \gg L_{\mathrm{cls}}$.
We verify this empirically: on CIFAR-100, IFCA with Oracle-provided correct initialisation (eliminating $\V$ entirely) still has $\eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})} = 0.40$ vs.\ FPT's $\eta_C = 0.002$ (Appendix~\ref{app:clean_eta}).
Setting $\kappa = \kappa_1$ completes the proof.

\textbf{Remark.} The theorem's primary contribution is Part~(i): within the stated regime, stale-initialisation amplification is a first-order property independent of fingerprint quality.
When $\V(w^{(\mathrm{init})}) = 0$ (no recurrence), the amplification gap vanishes and the advantage of Scheme~C rests solely on the $\eta_C \le \eta_A^{(\mathrm{clean})}$ condition, whose practical magnitude depends on the specific data and clustering algorithm.

\begin{lemma}[Gaussian Clustering Error Bound, two-class case]
\label{lem:gaussian_clustering}
For a two-class Gaussian mixture with equal priors, mean separation $\delta$ and per-class covariance $\sigma^2 I$, the Bayes-optimal clustering error is $\eta = \Phi(-\delta/(2\sigma))$, which is monotonically increasing in $\sigma^2$.
Adding isotropic variance $v$ to each class (i.e., replacing $\sigma^2$ by $\sigma^2 + v$) increases the error by at least $\kappa_1 v$, for $\kappa_1 = \frac{\delta}{4\sigma^3\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp(-\delta^2/(8\sigma^2))$, to first order in $v$.
For $C \ge 3$ classes with pairwise minimum separation $\delta$, the same variance-monotonicity holds with a constant $\kappa_1$ that may depend on $C$; we invoke only the two-class version in the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:protocol}.
\end{lemma}


\begin{lemma}[Information Indirectness under the Gaussian Linear Model]
\label{lem:info_indirectness}
Under the canonical model ($y = \langle \wstar_j, x \rangle + \varepsilon$, $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$), one gradient step from the correct initialisation $\wbar_j^{(t-1)}$ gives:
\[
\Delta_k = -\alpha \bigl(X_k^\top X_k\, \wbar_j^{(t-1)} - X_k^\top y_k\bigr) / n,
\]
where $X_k \in \R^{n \times d}$ is the local feature matrix. The concept signal in $\Delta_k$ is embedded in the quadratic form $X_k^\top X_k$, which has spectral noise of order $O(\sqrt{d/n})$ per eigenvalue.

In contrast, the class-conditional fingerprint $\phi_k = [\bar x_k, h_k, \bar x_{k,1}, \ldots, \bar x_{k,L_{\mathrm{cls}}}]$ encodes concept identity as a \emph{linear} function of the data distribution moments. Its effective clustering SNR under Gaussian classes is:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SNR}_{\mathrm{fp}} = \frac{\beta^2}{\sigma_{\mathrm{fp}}^2} \ge \frac{\delta^2}{\sigma^2/(n L_{\mathrm{cls}})},
\end{equation}
where $\delta$ is inter-concept separation and $L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ is the number of classes (notation distinct from the Lipschitz constant~$L$ of~(A1)). The weight-space SNR scales as $\delta^2 / (\sigma^2 d / n)$, which is a factor of $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ smaller. For $d \gg L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ (typical: $d = 128$, $L_{\mathrm{cls}} = 10$), this yields a substantial SNR advantage for fingerprints, supporting Assumption~(A5).
\end{lemma}

\textbf{Discussion.}
Lemma~\ref{lem:info_indirectness} does not prove (A5) in full generality---the weight update $\Delta_k$ in principle contains the full gradient information, and a sufficiently powerful clustering algorithm might extract concept identity from it.
The lemma shows that under the stated model, the \emph{natural} clustering metric (norm-based, as in A4) faces a $d/L_{\mathrm{cls}}$ disadvantage.
We treat (A5) as an explicit assumption, supported by the theoretical calculation above and by direct empirical measurement (Appendix~\ref{app:clean_eta}).

\section{Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit} (Implicit Shrinkage from Imperfect Clustering)}
\label{app:proof_implicit}
% =============================================================================

We establish the population-level contamination identity underlying Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit}: under symmetric misassignment at rate $\eta$, the concept-level estimator's expected value equals a shrinkage of the true concept parameter toward the global mean, with coefficient $\lambda_{\mathrm{imp}} = \rho := \eta C/(C-1)$.

\textbf{Setup.}
Fix a round $t$ and a concept $j \in [C]$. Under balanced concepts ($|\cC_j| = K/C$) and symmetric misassignment at rate $\eta$, each client's cluster label is drawn independently: given true concept $\ell$, the client is assigned to $\hat\cC_j$ with probability $1-\eta$ if $\ell = j$, and with probability $\eta/(C-1)$ otherwise. Let $N_{j,\ell} := |\{k \in \hat\cC_j : c(k,t) = \ell\}|$ be the (random) count of true-concept-$\ell$ clients inside $\hat\cC_j$, and let $N_j := \sum_\ell N_{j,\ell} = |\hat\cC_j|$. Each client's OLS estimate satisfies $\E[\what_k \mid c(k,t) = \ell] = \wstar_\ell + O(\sigma^2 d / n)$ under the Gaussian linear model of Section~\ref{sec:prelim}.

\textbf{Step 1: Contamination in expectation (conditional on composition).}
Write $\what_j := N_j^{-1}\sum_{k \in \hat\cC_j} \what_k$. Conditional on the composition $(N_{j,\ell})_{\ell}$ with $N_j > 0$,
\begin{equation}
\E\bigl[\what_j \,\big|\, (N_{j,\ell})_\ell\bigr] = \frac{1}{N_j}\sum_{\ell} N_{j,\ell}\,\wstar_\ell + O(\sigma^2 d/n).
\label{eq:contam_cond}
\end{equation}
Taking expectation over $(N_{j,\ell})$ and using $\E[N_{j,\ell}/N_j \mid N_j > 0] \to (1-\eta)$ for $\ell = j$ and $\eta/(C-1)$ for $\ell \ne j$ in the limit $K \to \infty$ (a standard i.i.d.\ ratio-of-binomials convergence, with rate $O(K^{-1/2})$ by the delta method), we obtain
\begin{align}
\E[\what_j]
&= (1-\eta)\,\wstar_j + \frac{\eta}{C-1}\sum_{\ell \ne j}\wstar_\ell + O(K^{-1/2}) \nonumber\\
&= (1-\eta)\,\wstar_j + \frac{\eta}{C-1}\bigl(C\,\wbar^* - \wstar_j\bigr) + O(K^{-1/2}) \nonumber\\
&= (1-\rho)\,\wstar_j + \rho\,\wbar^* + O(K^{-1/2}), \qquad \rho := \frac{\eta C}{C-1}.
\label{eq:contam_mean}
\end{align}
In the population limit $K \to \infty$ the remainder vanishes, giving the contamination identity: $\E[\what_j] = (1-\rho)\,\wstar_j + \rho\,\wbar^*$---precisely James--Stein--style shrinkage with coefficient $\lambda_{\mathrm{imp}} = \rho$ toward $\wbar^*$~\citep{james1961estimation, efron1973stein}.

\textbf{Step 2: Bias contribution (population limit, $\Sigma = I_d$).}
In the population limit, under $\Sigma = I_d$,
\begin{equation}
\|\E[\what_j] - \wstar_j\|^2 = \rho^2 \|\wstar_j - \wbar^*\|^2 = \rho^2 B_j^2.
\label{eq:contam_bias}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 3: Asymptotic crossover under contamination.}
In the population limit $K \to \infty$ with $Kn \to \infty$ and $d$ fixed, combining~(\ref{eq:contam_bias}) with the leading-order variance $\sigma^2 C d / (Kn)$ of Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition} yields the asymptotic excess risk $\rho^2 B_j^2 + \sigma^2 C d/(Kn) + o(1)$; comparing with the global-estimator risk $B_j^2 + \sigma^2 d/(Kn) + o(1)$ of Proposition~\ref{prop:decomposition}, concept-level aggregation dominates iff
\begin{equation}
(1 - \rho^2)\,B_j^2 > \frac{\sigma^2 d (C-1)}{Kn},
\qquad\text{equivalently}\qquad
\SNR > \frac{C-1}{1 - \rho^2},
\label{eq:contam_cross}
\end{equation}
recovering Eq.~(\ref{eq:noisy-crossover}) of Appendix~\ref{app:extensions}.

\textbf{Scope and honest caveats.}
Eq.~(\ref{eq:contam_mean}) is \emph{exact in the population limit} under per-client OLS unbiasedness and i.i.d.\ misassignment; the shrinkage identity $\lambda_{\mathrm{imp}} = \rho$ holds at this population level. Eq.~(\ref{eq:contam_bias}) additionally requires isotropy ($\Sigma = I_d$). Finite-sample corrections arise from three sources: (a) random cluster-size fluctuations of $N_j$ around $K/C$ (binomial, controllable via Bernstein's inequality), (b) random composition fluctuations of $N_{j,\ell}/N_j$ around their limits (delta-method $O(K^{-1/2})$), and (c) per-client OLS estimation error $O(\sigma^2 d/n)$ inside Step 1. Controlling all three to produce a tight finite-$Kn$ two-sided bound is beyond the scope of this corollary; we therefore state Corollary~\ref{cor:implicit} and Eq.~(\ref{eq:contam_cross}) as \emph{asymptotic / first-order characterisations}, and leave the fully finite-sample analysis to future work.

\section{Crossover Extensions}
\label{app:extensions}
% =============================================================================

The base crossover condition $\SNR > C{-}1$ extends to four practically relevant relaxations.
We state each modified crossover below; full proofs (and explicit regime assumptions) appear in the supplementary material.
The first three (unbalanced proportions, noisy concept labels, proportional high-dimensional regime) are rigorous under the stated regimes; the anisotropic-covariance threshold (Eq.~\ref{eq:deff-crossover}) and the unified formula (Eq.~\ref{eq:unified}) should be read as \emph{first-order heuristic combinations} of the individual thresholds under a spiked-covariance model, and not as proven exact thresholds.

\textbf{I.\;Unbalanced proportions.}
Let concept $j$ contain fraction $\pi_j$ of clients ($\sum_j \pi_j = 1$).
The proportion-weighted bias is $B_{j,\pi}^2 := \|w_j^* - \bar w^*_\pi\|^2$ with $\bar w^*_\pi = \sum_\ell \pi_\ell w_\ell^*$.
Concept-level aggregation wins for concept $j$ iff
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unbal-crossover}
\frac{Kn \cdot B_{j,\pi}^2}{\sigma^2 d} > \frac{1 - \pi_j}{\pi_j}.
\end{equation}
When $\pi_j = 1/C$ this recovers $\SNR > C{-}1$.
A rare concept ($\pi_j \ll 1$) faces threshold $\approx 1/\pi_j$: concept-level aggregation is harder to justify for minority groups.

\textbf{II.\;Noisy concept labels.}
Under symmetric label noise $\eta$ (each client is misassigned with probability $\eta/(C{-}1)$ to each wrong concept), define effective contamination $\rho = \eta C/(C{-}1)$.
The concept-level estimator converges to a $\rho$-mixture $\tilde w_j = (1{-}\rho) w_j^* + \rho \bar w^*$, yielding
\begin{equation}\label{eq:noisy-crossover}
\SNR > \frac{C - 1}{1 - \rho^2}.
\end{equation}
When $\eta > (C{-}1)/C$ ($\rho > 1$), concept-level aggregation is \emph{strictly dominated} at all SNR levels.
This gives a principled maximum tolerable label noise: $\eta_{\max} = \frac{C-1}{C}\sqrt{1 - (C{-}1)/\SNR}$.

\textbf{III.\;Proportional high-dimensional regime.}
When $d/(Kn) \to \gamma_G \in (0,1)$ and $dC/(Kn) \to \gamma_C \in (0,1)$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:prop-crossover}
\SNR > \frac{C - 1}{(1 - C\gamma_G)(1 - \gamma_G)}.
\end{equation}
Both factors in the denominator are $< 1$, so high dimensionality \emph{raises} the threshold, favouring global aggregation.
Concept-level OLS requires $Kn > Cd$ to be feasible at all.

\textbf{IV.\;Anisotropic covariance (heuristic).}
With features $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma)$ and effective rank $r_{\mathrm{eff}} = \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma)^2 / \|\Sigma\|_F^2$, a first-order heuristic in the spiked-covariance regime suggests that concept separation in the signal subspace yields a threshold of the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:deff-crossover}
\frac{Kn \cdot B_{j,\Sigma}^2}{\sigma^2 r_{\mathrm{eff}} \cdot s} \gtrsim C - 1,
\end{equation}
where $s$ is the dominant signal eigenvalue and $B_{j,\Sigma}^2 = \|\Sigma^{1/2}(w_j^* - \bar w^*)\|^2$.
Replacing $d$ by $r_{\mathrm{eff}} \ll d$ is expected to inflate the effective SNR. This is a heuristic (not a proven exact threshold): as discussed in the supplementary material, the prediction-risk variance in the classical regime remains $\sigma^2 d / N$ to leading order, so Eq.~(\ref{eq:deff-crossover}) should be read as an order-of-magnitude guide, to be tightened under an explicit spiked model.
In our CIFAR-100 experiments, $r_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 22 \ll d = 128$.

\textbf{Unified crossover (heuristic combination).}
Combining all four relaxations as a first-order heuristic, concept-level aggregation is expected to dominate for concept $j$ whenever
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unified}
\frac{\pi_j K n \cdot B_{j,\pi,\Sigma}^2}{\sigma^2 r_{\mathrm{eff}}}
\;\gtrsim\; \frac{1 - \pi_j}{(1 - \rho^2)(1 - \gamma_j)(1 - \pi_j\gamma_j)},
\end{equation}
where $\gamma_j = r_{\mathrm{eff}} / (\pi_j K n)$.
Under the base-case assumptions ($\pi_j{=}1/C$, $\eta{=}0$, $\Sigma{=}I_d$, $\gamma_j{\to}0$) this reduces to $\SNR > C{-}1$. Eq.~(\ref{eq:unified}) is a heuristic composition of the individual thresholds and is not claimed as an exact crossover; the individual thresholds (I)--(III) are rigorous under the stated regimes, while (IV) and the combined formula are heuristic (see supplementary material).

\section{fMoW Evaluation Protocol}
\label{app:fmow_protocol}
% =============================================================================

On CIFAR-100, CIFAR-10, and Fashion-MNIST, we use held-out test pools (20\% stratified split per concept, disjoint from training at the source-sample level).
On fMoW, we retain the standard prequential-on-same-pool protocol used by prior drift benchmarks~\citep{jothimurugesan2023federated, panchal2023flash} for the following reason.

\textbf{Why held-out is problematic for fMoW.}
fMoW's concept pools are constructed by year-range binning of the WILDS~\citep{koh2021wilds} training set.
With 62 classes and 4 year-range concepts, each concept pool contains ${\sim}600$ samples (${\sim}10$ samples per class).
A 20\% held-out split reduces the per-concept training pool to ${\sim}480$ samples (${\sim}8$ per class)---severely underdetermined for a 62-class linear classifier.
Under these conditions, Oracle (true per-concept aggregation) \emph{underperforms} FedAvg by ${\sim}7$pp because per-concept models undertrain relative to pooled models, and the concept-specific year-range signal does not compensate for the $4\times$ data disadvantage.
Since the purpose of fMoW in our benchmark is to test natural temporal drift (where concept boundaries are soft), not to demonstrate Oracle superiority, we report fMoW under the prequential protocol where per-concept models have access to the full pool.
